Time zones: why you shouldn't use the terms EST or GMT

Austin Tripp

2025-07-13

Source

This post is about a small mistake in how people discuss time zones which I find mildly annoying: using the term EST (Eastern Standard Time) to refer to the current time in cities like New York or Toronto, and GMT (Greenwich Mean Time) to refer to the current time in the UK. Unfortunately these terms are not equivalent due to daylight saving time.

There are only two ways that time zones can work with daylight saving time: either the time zone itself moves back/forward by 1h, or places change their time zone. I suppose when time zones were being set up either option could have been used, but the option used in western countries is the second option, ie that time zones stay fixed and cities move between time zones. This means that New York is not in EST in the summer (it is in EDT), and London is not in GMT (it is in BST).

A more unambiguous way to refer to local times is to just use a city / region / country name, eg "New York time", "Eastern Time", "UK time".

Why do I find this small thing annoying? Mostly because:

  1. If you type a time as written into a computer or digital calendar, you will get the wrong time!1
  2. It is a very small change to be correct (eg writing "ET" instead of "EST")

My ask: in the US/Canada, you can refer to time zones as PT/MT/CT/ET, not PST/MST/CST/EST. Refer to time in the UK as simply "UK time" or something similar.

  1. Some calendars seem to correct for this but not always, eg "GMT" is a common reference time zone all year and therefore likely won't be autocorrected. ↩
